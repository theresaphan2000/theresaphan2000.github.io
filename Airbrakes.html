CLARK AEROSPACE-AIRBRAKES AND MISC

Github Source
https://github.com/theresaphan2000/airbrakes

As the recovery finished sooner than expected, I later hopped onto airbrakes due to lack of team members. The reason we installed airbrakes was because we wanted to be able to control the speed of the rocket. Our main goal was to reach exactly 10k feet, and airbrakes would take care of one of our main problems which would be overshooting the rocke over apogee. 

AIRBRAKES SYSTEM

For the third-scale, we had functioning brakes with two variations of code. Our intentions with this was to test both variations of code to see which would give us the most accurate control over the rocket speed to meet our target. As for the full-scale, the previous year's design was implemented and manufactured. An acme screw is used for linear movement to control the 3 external flaps on the rocket with a stepper motor. The airbrakes are primarily controlled with Arduino that will read altitude and speed data as well. 

Issues and Solutions

Though we know that the airbrakes can be controlled and react the way it should in terms of increasing speed and decreasing speed, we never had the chance to test launch the rocket with the airbrakes on due to the pandemic. Despite so, here are the many problems we encountered and how we fixed the issue. 

PROBLEMS SOLUTIONS
Space limitations for powering Arduino and stepper motors for third scale. Our recovery system used an external battery, and after calculating the needed voltage for the altimeter, we realized we could sufficient power other components like airbrakes and its sensors as well. More details are on the recovery systems.
More space limitations and controlling airbrakes.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      One of our goals with airbrakes was to control how far out the external flaps would open. However, the previous years design did not implement a stop system for when the brakes were fully open, meaning the sever motor could spin infinitely, breaking the rocket components. We initially were going to input switches that would trigger when the airbrakes were fully open, however there was no space within the rocket.                                                                                                                                                                To fix this, we designed the switch itself into the lead screw and airbrake system. We attatched a screw into the airbrake flap design, and 2 washers at each end of the lead screw and grounded them. Our screw, is attatched to a voltage source, and when our airbrakes fully open or close, contract will be made between the screw and the washer, which will be read by our Arduino, stopping the motor from continuing to close or open indefintely. 
Calculating the velocity in the program The sensor the team chose to use did not include velocity as one of its measurements, which essential to our code. We initial tried to calculate the velocity equation on our own, however it became too complex due to other unknown forces, one of them being drag which could change depending on the orientation of the rocket, altitude, and other atmospheric changes. We decided to use Rocketsim to generate our data , which considered temperature, altitude, wind speed, and even drag force. Using the data, we exported it into excel, plotted the data points and found our velocity equation that way instead. That way, with changes in temperature and other variables, we could easly resimulate the rocket and change the velocity equation as needed.
