CLARK AEROSPACE-RECOVERY SYSTEM

Github Source
https://github.com/theresaphan2000/airbrakes

During my sophomore year of Clark College, I joined the school's aerospace team in preperation for the 2020 national ESRA rocket competition. Our main mission for this rocket was to reach  10k feet apogee. During my time working on the rocket, I specialized in the recovery system and the airbrake desgin of the rocket with one other team mate.

With the shut down, not only was the competition cut short and cancelled, we were also only able to finish and test launch our third scale model and made brief progress with the full-scale model itself. Despite the many disruptions that the COVID pandemic caused, we made incredible progress for our third scale model and hope to implement many of the designs and code into next years competition. 

Recovery System

This is a video of the test launch we did! I was very excited during this, so please excuse my commentary and for filming vertically!

The recovery system was a dual deployment system controlled by Telemetrum altimeters. Our drogue charges will be triggered by the altimeter, which will then ignite and trigger our parachute ejections.  For our full-scale rocket, the primary drouge ejection was to occur at apogee(our 10k goal)  and trigger a secondary backup drogue charge 5 seconds later. The 3ft drogue is housed in a  4 inch diameter, 22 inch fiberglass tube. 

Following the initial ejection, the rocket will descend to 1000ft, which is when our 12ft main drogue deploys.
The main is located in the 6inch upper body tube with 50ft of tubular nylon shock cords that are attached to either side of the AV bay eye nuts. Simultaneously, the Telemetrum altimeter will be sending GPS data, velocity, altitude, and other data information on the status of the rocket and its enviroment.

Issues and Solutions

To get to the test launch phase, we encountered several different issues. Here are the most significant problems and its solutions explained below.

PROBLEMS SOLUTIONS
Altimeter triggers ignitor, but not enough to light the black powder. We concluded with a multimeter that the voltage output of the altimeter was not enough to tigger the matches. We then attatched an external 3 volt lithium battery to the device to increase the output from the screw in terminals.
Questionable instances where the ignitors triggered unexpected and caused suprising explosions. Though a one time occurrence, it was a major safety hazard that would have been extremely dangerous for the third-scale and full-scale.                                                                                                    We couldn't figure out what was causing the ignitors to trigger even when the alitimeter was turned off/unplugged. Our connections were secure, no shorts were found etc. Since we shared the external battery with other components on the rocket such as airbrakes and other sensors, we tested the altimeter again with those plugged, unplugged, and individually connected but no issue was found. With that, we added a pull down resistor to the charge terminals and that fixed our main issue. 
Since airbrakes and the altimeter shared the same battery source, we needed to be able to safely eject the parachutes without doing damage to the components when the rocket splits open.  This was a third-scale model specific issue due to space limitations. Our sensors and microcontrollers were all actual components we were to use on the actual rocket, so fitting everything in was a major issue. Our airbrake system and other sensors were wired to the same external battery as the altimeter. During the drogue deployment, our nose cone splits from the rest of the rocket body which causes issues with the wire attatchments. To fix this, the rocket was  modified and rewired so that when the ejection does occur, the wiring would safetly disconnect exactly at the nose cone split.
Parachute melted during drogue deployment due to the black powder. We lightly wrapped the parachute in a damp cloth which worked really well. For our full-scale, we purchased "dog barf", also known as recovery padding to prevent the same issue.
